Should You Redesign Your Website or Start From Scratch?

Your site isn't working the way it used to. But does that mean you need a fresh coat of paint — or a whole new house? Here's how to know which one saves you money and which one wastes it.

April 17, 2026 5 min read By Will C.

At least once a month, someone reaches out and says some version of the same thing: "My website feels outdated and I don't know if I should fix it or just start over."

It's a fair question. And the answer matters, because one path costs you a few hundred dollars and a couple of weeks. The other costs you a few thousand and a month or more. Pick the wrong one and you either waste money rebuilding something that didn't need to be rebuilt, or you keep pouring time into a site that was broken at the foundation.

Here's how I walk clients through that decision.

Signs it's time for a change

Before you decide between a redesign and a rebuild, make sure you actually need either one. Not every website that feels old is actually hurting your business. But if you're seeing any of these, something needs to happen:

  • Your site isn't mobile-friendly. Over 60% of web traffic comes from phones. If your site pinches and scrolls sideways on mobile, you're losing people before they even read a word.
  • It loads slowly. If it takes more than three seconds, most visitors leave. That's not an opinion — it's what the data shows.
  • You're embarrassed to share the link. If you hand someone a business card and hope they don't actually visit your site, that's a problem.
  • Your content is outdated. Old pricing, discontinued services, a team page with people who left two years ago. It makes you look like you're not paying attention.
  • You're not getting leads or calls from it. A website that doesn't generate business is just an expense. It should be working for you.

If two or more of those hit home, keep reading. The next question is what kind of fix you actually need.

When a redesign is enough

A redesign means you keep the bones of your current site and update what's on the surface. New visuals, better copy, improved layout, modern fonts and colors. You're not tearing down the house — you're renovating it.

A redesign is the right call when:

  • Your site loads reasonably fast (under 4 seconds)
  • It's already mobile-responsive, just not great-looking
  • The page structure makes sense — you just need better content and design
  • Your site is built on a decent platform (clean HTML/CSS, a solid Shopify theme, a well-maintained WordPress setup)
  • You mainly need updated branding, photography, or messaging

In this case, a full rebuild would be overkill. You'd be paying to replace things that already work. A redesign gives you a modern, professional look without the time and cost of starting from zero.

I've done plenty of these. Sometimes a client comes to me thinking they need a brand new site, and after I look at what they have, I tell them the truth: "Your structure is fine. Let me just make it look and read like it belongs in 2026." That honesty saves them money, and they remember it.

When you need to start fresh

A rebuild means scrapping the existing site and building a new one from the ground up. New code, new design, new structure. It takes longer and costs more, but sometimes it's the only option that makes sense.

You need a rebuild when:

  • Your site was built on a drag-and-drop builder (Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy) and you've hit the ceiling on what it can do
  • The codebase is a mess — bloated plugins, broken scripts, slow load times that can't be fixed with optimization alone
  • Your business has changed significantly. New services, new audience, new brand positioning that the current site structure can't support
  • You have no mobile responsiveness at all, and retrofitting it would take as long as starting over
  • Your site was built five or more years ago with outdated technology

If the foundation is cracked, painting the walls won't help. A clean start lets you build something faster, leaner, and designed specifically for where your business is now — not where it was when the old site went live.

What each option costs

Redesign

  • Update visuals + content
  • Faster turnaround

Full Rebuild

A redesign typically runs $300 to $1,500 depending on how many pages need updating and how much content needs rewriting. A full rebuild ranges from $1,500 to $5,000+ depending on scope, features, and complexity. Check my pricing page for exact numbers on what I charge.

The mistake I see most often? Businesses paying rebuild prices for what should have been a redesign. An agency tells them they need a "complete overhaul" when what they actually need is new copy, a few layout tweaks, and some updated images. That's not a $5,000 job. Don't let someone convince you otherwise.

Most businesses don't need a brand new website every two years. They need someone to look at what they have and tell them the truth about what's working.

How to get started

If you've read this far, you probably already have a gut feeling about which direction is right for your site. Here's how to confirm it:

  1. Run a quick audit. Open your site on your phone. Time how long it takes to load. Click through every page. Does anything look broken, slow, or embarrassing? Write it down.
  2. Check your analytics. If you have Google Analytics set up, look at your bounce rate, average session duration, and which pages people actually visit. If they're landing on your homepage and immediately leaving, the problem is bigger than design.
  3. Get an honest opinion. Not from your friend who "knows computers." From someone who builds websites for a living and will tell you the truth, even if the truth is "you don't need to spend as much as you think."
